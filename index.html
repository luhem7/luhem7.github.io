Rust, simulations, and the question of how local rules turn into global behavior.

In the header above is Conway's Game of Life. Four little rules (two if you write them as B3/S23), no plan, no orchestrator, and it does all of that. Honestly, most of what I find interesting works this way: simple local rules, complicated emergent behavior.

I'm a Director of Analysis in Brokerage Architecture at Fidelity, based in North Carolina. Which is a fancy way of saying I think about how money actually moves under the hood (trades, settlement, all the plumbing nobody sees).

I did not follow a traditional path to get here. I started in QA, automated myself out of that job and into software, wandered through data engineering and data science (people analytics, then demand forecasting and logistics simulation over at Shopify), and eventually landed in architecture. There's no grand plan in that arc. I just follow whatever I'm curious about, and lately that's been building systems that allow for complex behavior without descending into chaos.

Currently building: zettledown, a Zettelkasten-flavored Markdown superset (Markdown, plus cards). Also the small web experiments linked below.

Off-hours I'm usually deep in Cosmere lore, rebuilding a Factorio base I swore would be the last one, following an F1 season I have too many opinions about, or rock climbing. Also, I love tinkering with cellular automata and procedural generation (the thing igniting up in the header), and building AI agents that define themselves.
